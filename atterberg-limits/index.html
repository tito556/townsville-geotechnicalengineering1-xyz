Atterberg Limits Testing in Townsville: Plasticity & Soil Classification

Technical studies that support your project.

LEARN MORE

Up here in Townsville the soil will catch you out if you don't check the plasticity. You've got those weathered residual clays from the granite and metasediments, plus the coastal alluvium near Ross Creek, and a lot of it shifts volume like crazy with the wet and dry seasons. A simple bore log doesn't tell you enough. We run Atterberg limits testing to AS 1289.3 because the liquid limit and plastic limit give you the real story on how the ground will behave under load. When the monsoonal rains hit and then the dry winter follows, knowing your plasticity index is what separates a slab that stays put from one that cracks. For deeper stratigraphy we often combine the lab work with a test pit investigation to log the profile visually before sampling.

The plasticity index is not just a number on a lab sheet — it is the single best predictor of ground movement in Townsville's reactive clay zones.

Methodology and scope

The Casagrande apparatus we use is calibrated every month against a reference soil, and the technician who runs the liquid limit test has handled Townsville clays for over a decade. The trick with our local material is patience during the thread-rolling for the plastic limit — the weathered granite fines can fool you into thinking you have hit the crumbling point too early if you rush it. We oven-dry everything at 105 °C and work the sample through the 425 μm sieve methodically, because the fines fraction is where the entire shrink-swell story lives. For projects near the Bohle River or the floodplain corridors, the linear shrinkage value often correlates tightly with the trouble the site will give you later, and we flag it early in the lab report. For pavement work, this data feeds directly into the CBR road subgrade assessment to make sure the pavement design accounts for the real soil behavior.

Local considerations

The classic mistake we see with smaller residential builds in Townsville is skipping the lab and relying on a generic soil classification from a visual log. Someone calls a sandy clay 'inert' and pours a standard slab, then two years later the walls are sticking and the tiles are popping. The culprit is almost always a plasticity index that was never measured. A soil can look sandy but carry enough clay fines to push the PI above 20%, well into reactive territory. The other error is not sampling deeply enough — the active zone here can extend beyond 1.5 metres. If you only test the fill or topsoil, you miss the real problem layer underneath. Running the full liquid limit and plastic limit set on samples from the foundation depth is not optional, it is the minimum standard for a durable structure in North Queensland.

Frequently asked questions

Why do Atterberg limits matter so much for Townsville soils specifically?

Townsville sits on a lot of moderately to highly reactive clay derived from weathered granite and coastal deposits. The plasticity index directly correlates with the ground's shrink-swell potential. When we get those big wet-season downpours followed by extended dry periods, the soil expands and contracts. Without knowing the Atterberg limits, a footing designed generically is a gamble. AS 2870 classifies sites based on this data, and we see sites here that push into H1 and H2 reactivity classes regularly.

How much does Atterberg limits testing cost in Townsville?

For standard Atterberg determination on a single sample, you are looking at a range of AU$110 to AU$160 depending on the exact suite of tests requested and whether linear shrinkage is included. We always quote per sample with clear breakdowns before the work starts.

What is the difference between liquid limit and plastic limit?

The liquid limit is the moisture content at which the soil changes from a plastic state to a liquid state — essentially where it starts to flow under its own weight. The plastic limit is the moisture content where the soil transitions from a semi-solid state to a plastic one, defined as the point where a 3 mm thread crumbles. The gap between these two values is the plasticity index, and that number tells your engineer how much trouble the soil can cause during wet-dry cycles.

Do you need a specific type of sample for the Atterberg test?

We can work with disturbed bag samples for most of the Atterberg suite, which makes the sampling process straightforward during a site investigation. The key is that the sample must be representative of the stratum in question and kept sealed to retain its natural moisture if you also want field moisture content correlated. Samples are processed initially by drying and sieving through a 425 μm sieve, as per AS 1289.3.
