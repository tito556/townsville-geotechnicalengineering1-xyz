Methodology and scope

The sieve stack we deploy for Townsville projects starts with a 75 mm top screen and runs down to the 75-micron No. 200 pan — standard Australian mesh sizes that match Transport and Main Roads specification MRTS04. For the coarse fraction retained on each sieve, the technician dry-weighs to 0.1 g resolution after oven-drying at 105 °C. The minus-75-micron material does not get ignored: that entire pan fraction goes straight into a sodium hexametaphosphate dispersion and a 152H hydrometer cylinder for a timed sedimentation run at 20 °C. We read the hydrometer at 0.5, 1, 2, 4, 8, 15, 30, 60, 120, and 1440 minutes — the full ASTM D7928-compatible schedule — so the silt-clay boundary near 2 microns is resolved with enough data points to plot a smooth grading curve. Every Townsville sample gets its own temperature-compensated blank cylinder running alongside, because ambient lab temperatures in Rosslea in February can drift three degrees between the first and last reading. The combined sieve-plus-hydrometer report delivers D10, D30, D50, D60, and D85 values, the uniformity and curvature coefficients, and the percent gravel-sand-silt-clay breakdown that geotechnical engineers need for USCS classification and internal stability assessment of graded filters.

Local considerations

Townsville sits on a complex mix of Quaternary alluvium, deeply weathered granite, and coastal plain sediments. The Ross River floodplain deposits can carry 30–60% silt with intermittent clay lenses that standard bore logs often miss. If grain size analysis stops at the sieve stack and skips the hydrometer, the entire silt-clay fraction gets lumped as 'minus 75 micron' — useless for predicting capillary rise, frost heave potential, or the piping susceptibility of a levee fill. We have pulled samples from subdivision sites in Mount Louisa where the hydrometer revealed 18% clay content in material that the naked eye called 'silty sand'. That single data point changed the stormwater infiltration design from a standard pit to a lined retention system. The cost of excavation and replacement after a failed permeability test runs ten times the cost of a combined sieve-and-hydrometer analysis done during the pre-tender investigation. In a region where Cyclone Yasi-grade rainfall can deliver 300 mm in 24 hours, grading curves that lack the full fines tail are not a shortcut — they are a liability.

Frequently asked questions

How much does a combined sieve and hydrometer grain size analysis cost in Townsville?

For a full combined analysis covering the 75 mm to 1-micron range with the 24-hour hydrometer schedule, budget between AU$180 and AU$300 per sample. The price varies with the number of samples batched together and whether you need the standard 24-hour sedimentation or an accelerated 6-hour protocol for time-sensitive earthworks. Transport of samples from remote sites like Charters Towers or the Burdekin can add a small logistics fee.

How long does a hydrometer test take from sample receipt to final report?

The hydrometer sedimentation alone requires a minimum 24-hour reading series. With sample preparation, oven-drying, sieve shaking, and report drafting, the standard turnaround is 3 to 4 business days. We can compress this to 48 hours for urgent projects, but the 24-hour hydrometer reading cannot be shortened without sacrificing accuracy on the clay fraction below 2 microns.

Do I need the hydrometer if my material looks like clean sand?

In Townsville, yes — more often than you would expect. Weathered granite from the Mount Stuart foothills and alluvial sands from the Ross River can carry 8–15% silt that is invisible to the naked eye but sufficient to shift the USCS classification from SW to SM. That change affects compaction targets, permeability estimates, and the material's suitability as structural fill under MRTS04.

What sample mass do you need for a reliable grain size analysis?

It depends on the maximum particle size. For material with Dmax up to 20 mm, we need about 500 g of oven-dried material. For material containing gravel up to 75 mm, the required mass jumps to 5 kg to ensure the coarse fraction is statistically representative. We can advise on splitting procedures if you are sampling from a stockpile at a Townsville quarry or borrow pit.
