Field Permeability Testing (Lefranc & Lugeon) in Townsville

Technical studies that support your project.

LEARN MORE

A site in Rosslea sits on alluvial silts that drain overnight. Cross the river to Kirwan on granitic residual soils, and water lingers in the excavation for days. Same city, two permeability profiles that change the cost of a retaining wall or a basement slab. In Townsville, where the wet season can dump over a metre of rain between December and March, understanding how water moves through the ground is not a desktop exercise. The Lugeon test quantifies fracture flow in the granite and dolerite that underpin Castle Hill, while the Lefranc method gives us reliable point-source data in the sandy clays common across the Bohle Plains. We run both methods under AS 1726, feeding hydraulic conductivity values directly into dewatering design and foundation drainage plans.

A Lugeon value under 3 indicates tight rock where grouting may be unnecessary; above 10, you are dealing with open fractures that demand treatment before excavation.

Methodology and scope

The field kit we mobilise around Townsville pairs a double-packer system for Lugeon stages with a variable-head Lefranc setup for shallow test intervals. On a recent job near the Ross River, we ran Lugeon stages at five-metre intervals down to 25 m in the Townsville Dolerite, injecting water at controlled pressures while logging flow against time. The packers isolate each section so fracture permeability is measured, not guessed. For Lefranc testing in the overlying silty sand, we used a 115 mm casing with a 60 mm slotted screen, recording water level recovery with a 0.1 mm transducer. The CPT test data had already mapped the soft clay lenses, and the permeability values we derived from Lefranc matched the dissipation tests within 15%, which gave the civil contractor confidence to size the wellpoints. When the ground gets tricky, the same rig can switch to grouting if we encounter open fractures that would bias the test.

Local considerations

Townsville sits at the edge of the dry tropics. Six months of low rainfall followed by monsoonal bursts create a groundwater regime that swings pore pressures from near-zero to artesian in a matter of weeks. A permeability value measured in October, when the water table is at its lowest, may not represent the soil you will excavate in February. The weathered granite profile beneath many northern suburbs holds perched water in the upper two metres, while the underlying fractured rock can transmit flow from recharge zones kilometres away. Underestimating hydraulic conductivity here means pumps that cannot keep up, batter failures from seepage pressure, or a basement slab that floats. The field test captures this temporal and spatial variability in a way that laboratory permeameter tests on small samples simply cannot, and it provides the numbers that AS 4678 demands for drainage design behind retaining structures.

Frequently asked questions

What does a Lefranc or Lugeon permeability test cost in Townsville?

For a single test interval, budget between AU$940 and AU$1,560 depending on depth, access, and whether we are testing in soil (Lefranc) or rock (Lugeon). A full day of multi-interval testing with a drill rig typically runs higher due to the setup time and equipment. We provide fixed-price quotes after reviewing the borehole logs and site constraints.

When should I choose a Lugeon test over a laboratory permeameter test?

Choose the Lugeon test whenever the rock mass controls the hydraulic behaviour — think fractured dolerite, jointed granite, or weathered bedrock. A lab test on an intact core plug misses the fractures that carry 90% of the flow. The Lugeon method pressurises a section of the borehole and measures the bulk rock mass permeability directly, which is the value that matters for tunnel lining, dam foundation grouting, or deep excavation dewatering.

How long does field permeability testing take on a Townsville site?

A single Lefranc test in soil can be completed in one to two hours once the borehole is prepared. A full five-stage Lugeon test in rock at one interval takes about 90 minutes. For a typical investigation with four to six test intervals, plan on one full day onsite plus setup and demobilisation. We schedule work around the wet season where possible to avoid delays from surface flooding.
