MASW & VS30 Shear Wave Velocity Testing in Townsville

Technical studies that support your project.

LEARN MORE

Townsville's geotechnical profile shifts dramatically from the granite-derived soils of Castle Hill and Magnetic Island to the deep, soft alluvium of the Ross River floodplain. A conventional borehole can tell you the material type, but the real question for any mid-rise or essential facility here is how those layers transmit energy during a seismic event. Cyclone-prone coastal settings add another layer: saturated profiles and loose Holocene sediments can amplify ground motion in ways that standard SPT blow counts fail to capture. We use the Multichannel Analysis of Surface Waves to map shear-wave velocity profiles directly, delivering a measured VS30 that anchors your site class. When the alluvium extends beyond 30 metres, combining MASW with a seismic refraction survey helps constrain the deeper basement geometry that controls basin-edge effects.

A measured VS30 beats a generic site class every time — in Townsville's alluvial corridors, the difference between Class C and Class D can reshape an entire foundation design.

Methodology and scope

What we keep seeing in Townsville is that developers underestimate velocity contrasts between the weathered granite regolith and the overlying transported soils. A 24-channel geophone spread with a sledgehammer source can resolve those boundaries to within half a metre, provided you keep the array straight along a cleared pad. We follow the AS 1726 framework for site investigation and supplement it with NEHRP classification tables, because Australian standards don't define site classes the same way. The raw dispersion image is inverted to produce a 1D VS profile, and when lateral variability matters we run parallel lines to build a pseudo-2D cross-section. For project sites near the Bohle River or the Town Common, where the water table sits barely a metre down, the surface-wave method has a genuine advantage: saturated ground damps body-wave signals but leaves Rayleigh-wave dispersion largely intact.

Local considerations

A four-storey mixed-use project on the south bank of the Ross River taught us a lesson about skipping a velocity survey. The bore logs showed medium-dense sand to 12 metres, and the structural engineer assumed a Class C site by default. Construction started, and within three months the contractor hit saturated loose lenses at 8 metres that the CPT had only partially detected. A single MASW line run post-excavation returned a VS30 of 172 m/s, pushing the site firmly into Class D territory, which meant the lateral load demands on the moment frames had been underestimated. The remedial work — thicker shear walls and an additional row of piles — cost more than the original survey would have. Townsville sits in a moderate-hazard region under AS/NZS 1170.4, but the site amplification factor alone can double the design base shear when you move from the default class to the real measured value.

Frequently asked questions

What does a MASW test in Townsville typically cost?

For a standard active-source MASW survey with one or two lines, the fee generally falls between AU$2.650 and AU$4.560. The spread depends on how many array positions you need and whether passive-source recording is required to reach beyond 30 metres. We provide a fixed-price quote after reviewing your site plan and access conditions.

Is MASW accepted for site classification under Australian standards?

AS 1726 references geophysical methods for site characterisation, and the NEHRP site-class framework is widely adopted by structural engineers in Australia for defining the seismic design category. Our reports map the measured VS30 directly to the NEHRP class A through E, which aligns with the site-factor tables in AS/NZS 1170.4.

How long does a MASW survey take on site?

A single active-source line on reasonably cleared ground takes two to three hours, including setup of the 24-channel spread, multiple shot points for stacking, and a quick-look dispersion check. If we need to add a passive array for deeper velocity information, allow an extra hour of recording time.

Can MASW work on Magnetic Island's granite terrain?

It works, but with a different signature. The weathered granite profile produces a sharp velocity ramp from 400 m/s in the residual soil to over 1,500 m/s in the fractured rock. The dispersion image is clean, and inversion resolves the overburden thickness well, which is exactly what you need for footing set-out on the island's sloping blocks.
