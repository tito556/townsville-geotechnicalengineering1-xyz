Field Density Testing (Sand Cone Method) in Townsville – QA That Holds Up

Technical studies that support your project.

A recent warehouse extension in Bohle hit a snag last year. The structural fill met the spec on paper, but the first round of plate load tests showed uneven settlement across the pad. The contractor called us in for a forensic look. We ran a grid of sand cone tests at 300 mm depth intervals and found a 40 m² zone where compaction had dropped to 92% of modified maximum dry density — fine on its own, but the moisture was 3% above optimum thanks to a leaking irrigation main nobody had spotted. That is the reality of earthworks in Townsville’s coastal plains. The sand cone density test gives you a direct measurement of in-place density and moisture content, which no nuclear gauge can match when the fill contains variable crushed rock or slag from the copper refinery. We pair it with Proctor tests to establish the reference curve, and often recommend CBR testing when the formation level will carry heavy traffic.

A nuclear gauge gives you a number in 60 seconds. A sand cone test gives you a number you can defend in a claims meeting.

Methodology and scope

One thing you learn quickly working around the Bohle River floodplain is that Townsville’s residual soils do not behave like textbook materials. The weathered granite and meta-sediments can look competent in a test pit but break down rapidly under compaction rollers. The sand cone method, run to AS 1289.5.3.1, is the only field test that gives you both wet density and moisture content independently — no calibration curves, no assumptions about soil chemistry. We use calibrated Ottawa sand with a bulk density verified every 20 tests in our NATA-accredited lab. The procedure itself is straightforward: excavate a hole, collect all the spoil, weigh it, fill the void with sand from the cone apparatus, and calculate the volume. But the real skill is in selecting test locations that catch the edges of a lift, not just the easy middle. On a recent TMR subgrade job near the Bruce Highway, we combined sand cone testing with in-situ permeability measurements because the spec required a drainage layer that would not hold water during a wet season downpour.

Local considerations

Townsville sits on a complex mix of Quaternary alluvium, deeply weathered Palaeozoic granite, and coastal marine clays. The magnetic susceptibility of the local granite can throw off nuclear density gauges by 3–5%, a known issue in the Mount Stuart foothills. Relying solely on a nuclear gauge in those areas means you are either over-compacting and wasting fuel, or under-compacting and building in a settlement risk. We have also seen imported fill sourced from quarries near Charters Towers that contains up to 15% gypsum by mass — it looks like road base but dissolves under repeated wetting, leaving voids that a sand cone test will flag as low density long before the pavement fails. The risk is not theoretical. A 2019 warehouse slab in Garbutt required full-depth reconstruction because density records from nuclear testing did not match core samples, and the dispute went to adjudication. The sand cone method provides a physical volume measurement that is not affected by soil chemistry, ironstone nodules, or salinity, which makes it the preferred referee method under AS 3798 for contentious lots.

Frequently asked questions

How much does a sand cone density test cost in Townsville?

A single sand cone test typically runs between AU$140 and AU$210, depending on the number of tests per day and travel distance to the site. A full-day program with 8–12 tests and same-day moisture content determination is the most cost-effective setup for most earthworks projects.

Why use the sand cone method instead of a nuclear gauge?

The sand cone method measures volume directly by filling the excavation with calibrated sand, so it is unaffected by the magnetic minerals in Townsville’s granite or by variable ironstone content in fill. Nuclear gauges require calibration for each soil type and can give biased readings in chemically unusual materials. The sand cone is also the reference method specified in AS 3798 for referee testing when density results are disputed.

How many field density tests do I need per lift?

Under typical TMR MRTS04 guidelines, a minimum of one test per 500 m² per compacted lift applies for general fill. For structural fill under footings or slabs, we often recommend tightening to one test per 250 m². The key is distributing tests to cover edges, transitions between cut and fill, and any areas where moisture conditions changed during placement.

Can you test through coarse gravel or crushed rock?

Yes, but the hole volume must be large enough to be representative. For material with nominal particle size up to 37.5 mm, we excavate a hole approximately 200 mm diameter by 200 mm deep. For larger rock fill, the sand cone method becomes impractical and we recommend a water replacement method or test fill trials with survey control.
