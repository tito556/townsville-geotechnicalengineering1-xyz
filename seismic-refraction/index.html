Seismic Tomography (Refraction/Reflection) Surveys in Townsville

Technical studies that support your project.

LEARN MORE

Townsville sits on a complex geological fringe where weathered granites and coastal alluvium meet, and that mix can do strange things to a seismic signal. It is not unusual to find a stiff residual soil profile overlying a partially decomposed rock mass that looks competent but behaves unpredictably under load. Our approach to seismic tomography here leans heavily on the refraction method for mapping bedrock undulations across Castle Hill's flanks and the broader Bohle Plains, where paleochannels filled with soft clay can escape a standard drilling program. Reflection surveys help when depth penetration beyond 40 metres is needed, though the technique requires careful parameter selection to manage the high-attenuation surface layers common in the dry tropics. A solid velocity model upfront saves money later, and in a city growing as fast as Townsville, time on the drill rig is too expensive to waste.

A seismic tomogram in Townsville's weathered granite can reveal a bedrock depression that a borehole alone would miss entirely.

Methodology and scope

The magnetic declination shift and shallow groundwater variations across Townsville's suburbs, from Annandale to Douglas, influence how seismic energy propagates through the near-surface. In granitic terrain near Lavarack Barracks, P-wave velocities typically range from 400 m/s in residual soil to over 3000 m/s in fresh rock, giving a clear impedance contrast that refraction tomography picks up reliably.
We run 24- or 48-channel spreads with a sledgehammer or weight-drop source depending on target depth, processing the first breaks through a tomographic inversion that honours the curved ray paths expected in gradational weathering profiles. Where a borehole is available, we tie the velocity model to actual lithology, calibrating the tomogram with data from a test pit or a rotary core log so the engineering team gets more than just a colour contour. In deeper investigations for multistorey structures, combining the tomogram with MASW data provides both the compressional and shear-wave velocity structure required by AS 1170.4 for site classification, a detail that has caught out more than one rushed project in North Queensland.

Local considerations

A project on the Ross River floodplain taught a lesson worth sharing: three boreholes showed stiff clay to 12 metres, but a refraction line between them revealed a buried channel filled with loose saturated sand that was completely invisible in the logs. Without that tomogram, the pad footing design would have assumed uniform bearing and the differential settlement would have been severe. In Townsville, where post-cyclone reconstruction often accelerates timelines, skipping the velocity model during the site investigation phase introduces a risk that no amount of structural over-design can reliably cover. The problem is not just missing soft spots; it is over-estimating rippability in road cuttings and ending up with a contractor delay claim when the grader hits shallow rock that a refraction survey would have mapped with a few hours of field work.

Frequently asked questions

What does a seismic refraction survey in Townsville typically cost?

For a single 115-metre refraction line with 24 geophones and a sledgehammer source, budgets in Townsville usually fall between AU$4,140 and AU$7,750. The final figure depends on how many shots per spread, the need for a weight-drop source on harder ground, and the processing time required for tomographic inversion. Longer lines, multiple spreads, or reflection profiling push the cost toward the upper end or beyond.

How deep can seismic refraction see in North Queensland geology?

Depth penetration is tied to spread length, not the source energy alone. With a 100-metre spread, we reliably image to about 30–35 metres in Townsville's weathered granite and alluvial soils. If the target is deeper, a 150-metre spread or a switch to reflection acquisition is needed. The dry tropical surface layer here is notoriously attenuative, so we adjust the source and geophone coupling accordingly.

How do you calibrate the seismic velocity model with actual soil types?

Velocity is ambiguous by itself: a P-wave speed of 1800 m/s could be stiff clay, highly weathered granite, or cemented sand. We remove that ambiguity by tying the tomogram to at least one borehole or test pit log. Once the lithology is pinned at a known location, the velocity contours can be interpreted across the entire line with confidence, and the resulting ground model meets the AS 1726 requirement for a defensible geotechnical basis.

Can seismic tomography help classify the site for earthquake design?

Yes, but only when combined with a shear-wave velocity profile. Seismic refraction gives the P-wave velocity, and with a parallel MASW survey or downhole test we obtain the Vs30 needed for AS 1170.4 site classification. In Townsville, where the seismic hazard is moderate but the soil amplification potential on deep alluvium is real, getting both velocities right matters for structural design.
